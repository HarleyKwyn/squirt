    McConnell Vows a Senate in Working Order, if He Is Given Control

    By CARL HULSEMARCH 3, 2014

    Inside
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story Share This Page
      Continue reading the main story

      WASHINGTON — Senator Mitch McConnell still has a few obstacles to overcome to become Senate majority leader next year. First, he has to win his own primary and general election back in Kentucky, where his popularity has suffered. Then his fellow Republicans have to pick up at least six seats for the party to take over the Senate for the first time since 2006.

      It is not inconceivable at this point, and Mr. McConnell has been giving considerable thought to how he would run the Senate if given the chance. Spoiler alert: It is not how he thinks it is being run now.

      “We are going to treat senators with respect — we are going to work harder and accomplish more,” said Mr. McConnell, still furious with Senator Harry Reid of Nevada, the current majority leader, over what Republicans considered an outrageous power grab in limiting the filibuster last year. “The Senate can be returned to the place of great debates, contentious debates, but where you can still get outcomes on things where you have at least 60 senators.”

      As he looks ahead to the possibility of leading the Senate, Mr. McConnell is promising a more open floor, with senators from both parties able to offer amendments. He says committees would be given more independence and authority to advance legislation. While he would not commit to reversing the limit Democrats put on filibustering White House nominees last November, he said the idea would be on the table if Republicans took charge.

      “If the American people give us the opportunity to put the Senate under new management, it is an appropriate discussion at that time for the new team that would be taking over the Senate,” Mr. McConnell said in an interview. “It is a conversation for December.”

      The prospect of returning the threshold for breaking filibusters on nominees to 60 votes from the simple majority established by the Democrats in November does not represent an immediate risk for Republicans. If they seize the majority, they will not need to rely on the procedural tactic to block President Obama’s nominees during his final two years in office, since they will control the agenda. And Democrats in the minority presumably would not filibuster administration choices.

      But reversing the filibuster change would be a symbolic reproach over an act that Republicans contend did significant damage to the Senate — an institution that prides itself on strict adherence to custom and traditions — without getting Democrats all that much.

      Mr. McConnell’s gaze ahead also reflects the momentum Republicans feel in their push to regain the majority after getting a boost in Colorado last week with the entry of a stronger challenger, potentially putting a Democratic seat there in play.

      In making his case for a Republican majority, Mr. McConnell is also seeking to draw a contrast with Mr. Reid, who has come under intense Republican criticism for the tight control he has imposed on the Senate — control that Mr. Reid said was necessary because of record obstruction by Republicans in their push to derail Mr. Obama and his priorities.

      Given their recent experience with Mr. McConnell, Democrats scoff at his promises of Senate comity. They say that he has prided himself on holding Republicans together against Mr. Obama, that he helped scuttle a bipartisan transportation bill that emerged from the committee process he now says is underappreciated and that he has allowed more conservative Republicans to foul the Senate gears without intervening.

      “He has waged a strategy that was based on blocking things for political reasons, whatever the merits,” said Adam Jentleson, a spokesman for Mr. Reid. “There is no basis for any argument that he makes to improve the Senate, because his track record shows otherwise.”

      Mr. McConnell is not the first congressional leader to pledge, if given control, more fairness and a return to what is known on Capitol Hill as regular order.

      As House Democrats were poised to take back control in 2006, they promised a more inclusive floor process and consultation with the minority, but found themselves quickly backtracking as Republicans employed floor tactics that put Democrats on defense.

      When House Republicans regained control in 2010, the incoming speaker, John A. Boehner of Ohio, held out the prospect of unlimited amendments, time to digest bills and little late-night legislating. Those guarantees also fell to the political and practical realities of having to govern.

      Mr. McConnell, who in a speech at the beginning of the year acknowledged his own role in the polarization of the Senate, did not commit to an open-ended debate on every bill, but provided an outline of how he thinks he can make the Senate more functional. The key, he said, is to announce that debate on a bill will begin Monday and conclude by the end of the week, pressuring senators to step forward with their proposals.

      “If you say you are going to finish a bill and you mean it and you are willing to use the fatigue factor, you can get an outcome without having to jam people,” he said. “You would end up working harder, without the feeling that some dictator was trying to tell you what you could and couldn’t do.”

      He suggested that one of the most basic sources of sour relations in the Senate these days is the reluctance of Mr. Reid to allow a sufficient number of amendments in his effort to protect politically vulnerable members from tough votes. According to Mr. McConnell, members of both parties feel constrained.

      “It disrespects the members on both sides of the aisle,” he said. “I have heard from Democratic senators who wonder what they are supposed to do here, their committee work is not honored, they don’t get to offer amendments. What is the job of a senator?”

      The coming months will determine if Mr. McConnell’s next job as a senator is majority leader and whether he can then make good on his vision for turning around the Senate.
